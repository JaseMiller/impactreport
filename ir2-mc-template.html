Working together for integrated community transformation February 2017—SOUTH AFRICA

READ THE STORY

Parts of South Africa are some of the hardest places I've ever traveled to, but they're also places where Jesus is doing some truly amazing things. We bring people together to help the oppressed, even when it's extremely difficult. If things are going to change, collaborating within a community is a necessity.

We believe Jesus is making all things new... So, when we enter a community we want to see everyone in that community through the eyes of Jesus.

No one should be invisible. We want to be aware of leaders who are well-known in the community, but also people who get overlooked because they're at the margins of society. Whether those people are "invisible" intentionally or due to unconscious bias, we seek them out.

Most communities already have significant resources that are being overlooked or that aren't accessible to everyone. Discovering and working to overcome barriers that keep people from working together is a key part of changing the status quo.

When we discover people in communities—such as those that surround East London in South Africa—who are just as committed to this way of approaching transformative work, we naturally find ourselves coming alongside one another. Our working together is full of hope, and you're part of that. I hope that will be clear to you as you read this Impact Report.


Scott Olson
President & CEO

Sometimes the kind of change we long to see comes very slowly. Tangible external fruits can take a long time to grow out of preceding systemic (social, economic, and political) and individual (spiritual, psychological) changes. East London, a small city on the Indian Ocean coast of South Africa, is a case in point.

Characteristic of most cities in South Africa, extending outward from its periphery are many townships and rural settlements established in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. During the apartheid era in the mid- to late-1900s, these townships and settlements became spaces where non-white residents (including primarily black, "coloured" and Indian populations) were forcefully relocated in large numbers as part of a brutally oppressive national governmental policy. In some sectioned off regions, they were given a measure of autonomy for governing their own affairs, but this was vastly limited and subjected to the ruling minority class. All of this resulted in a large majority of the entire national population marginalized and silenced.

Imagine such a large group of people effectively imprisoned on small, infertile parcels of wasteland who are allowed to govern locally within their boundaries, but with no authority to expand or trade beyond their borders. Imagine how limited their resulting access is to quality health care, education, business and other vital resources. Imagine how families are torn apart as fathers have to leave and find exploitative work far away in dangerous mines, in order to earn a living and send money home.

Now imagine this continuing for multiple generations, as emigration patterns, social patterns and cultures harden and become difficult to change. Imagine the harmful social forces fostered during forced apartheid continuing, and even expanding, as a remnant to this day - more than 20 years after apartheid officially ended. It's not so unlike the policies enacted by the United States toward native peoples in North America for generations in the past, whose ill effects are still seen today.

This sounds like a bleak context, and in one sense it certainly is. But the city-wide network described in this impact report is all about building on an invaluable resource in the midst of such challenge—unknown people still living in such impoverished communities, standing up in the midst of it all and saying, "I believe in something much better, and I want to give my life to help others find it!"

Most of these heroic individuals lead tiny churches and/or organizations and have virtually no funding and few supportive staff. They're giving their lives and spending out of their own pockets to help their neighbours, who come knocking on their doors with any and every problem under the sun. The pressure to help the broken, the destitute, the hungry, the sick, the addicted, the victimized is immense. It's a lonely job. And yet faith in God, a vision of hope, and the joy, courage and patience that accompanies these, are vibrant and strong. This is what ultimately ties them together, across community lines to mutually support one another. This is what HOPE community mobilization network is all about.


Scott Worley
ITeams, South Africa
